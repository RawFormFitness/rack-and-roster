# The Rack & Roster Manifesto

A letter to traditional strength gym owners.

---

## What these gyms are

The room matters.

A traditional strength gym, a real one with iron, rubber, chalk, a squat rack that works, and someone behind the counter who knows the difference between a lifter and a tourist, is not a commodity. It's a specific kind of place, and in most of this country there are fewer of them every year.

I've spent a decade running the sales floor at some of Manhattan's most iconic independent strength gyms. The ones lifters fly in to train at. The ones that have been open longer than most of their members have been alive. The ones with names on the door that mean something.

These places built American lifting culture. They still carry it. And right now, most of them are in trouble. Not because the culture has faded, but because the economics underneath the culture have gotten harder, and the owners running them have been given almost no real tools to see their own businesses clearly.

This is a letter about that.

---

## What's actually happening

The demand for serious training hasn't gone anywhere. Walk into any real strength gym on a Tuesday night and the squat racks are full. The coaching is better than it's ever been. The community is tighter than any group fitness class will ever replicate. The product is strong.

The business infrastructure underneath the product is not.

Rent is up. Labor is up. Member acquisition costs more than it used to and produces weaker leads. Most traditional gym owners are running roughly the same business model they ran in 2015, and the 2015 model doesn't work in 2026. The gap between what the floor is producing and what the P&L is showing is widening quietly, month after month, and by the time most owners notice, they've already made a string of decisions they can't undo.

I'm going to tell you what I see, because I've seen it at multiple gyms now, and it's almost always the same story.

**Most traditional strength gyms don't know their real numbers.**

Not because the owners are lazy. Most of the owners I've worked with are some of the sharpest operators in any industry. They're lifters who built a business around something they love, and they run it harder than any corporate middle manager ever would.

They don't know their numbers because the tools they have don't tell them. The CRM says one thing. The POS says another. The front desk staff tag leads inconsistently, or tag them after the fact, or don't tag them at all. By the time the data gets to the owner, it's been filtered through three broken systems, and the reports coming out of those systems are answering the wrong questions.

Ask a gym owner which lead source is producing their best members and most of them will tell you with confidence. Pull the data the right way and half the time it's wrong. Not because they're guessing. Because the system lied to them, and they had no way to know.

**Lead volume is a trap.**

The number one way traditional gyms lose money right now is promos measured on lead volume instead of net impact.

A three-day free pass promo generates a lot of bodies through the door. The CRM lights up. The owner looks at the lead count and feels good. What the owner doesn't see is that the promo leads close at a third of the rate of baseline leads, the sales team burns a week of capacity running tours that don't convert, and the net new members at the end of the month are roughly identical to a month without the promo. Except now the staff is exhausted and the regulars are complaining about the floor being crowded with tourists.

I've watched this play out. I've measured it. It's not a one-off. It's a pattern that kills these businesses slowly, because every time the promo "works" on lead count, the owner runs it again.

**Retention is measured wrong.**

Most owners look at churn as a flat monthly percentage. "We lose 3% a month." That number hides more than it shows. If the member base is growing, 3% of a growing base means you're losing more members every month in absolute terms while the percentage stays flat. If a specific signup cohort is churning out faster than another, a flat number won't show it.

Cohort retention is the only honest way to look at this. What percentage of members who joined in a given month are still there six months later, twelve months later, eighteen months later. Almost no traditional gym tracks it. They can't. The reports don't exist.

**The consulting industry has failed this segment.**

Marketing agencies sell leads. Your business doesn't need more leads. Your business needs more members, and the gap between those two things is where the money is being lost.

The generic "scale your gym" consultants want to turn your operation into a system that looks like every other operation. The thing that makes your gym valuable, whether you're single-location or part of a larger name, is the culture on your floor. That doesn't scale by template. It scales by being protected.

And the people selling generic small-business advice don't know what a fair close rate is for a walk-in tour on a Saturday, don't know what a reasonable promo baseline looks like, and have never stood on a sales floor talking to a nervous new lifter about joining.

Nobody who actually understands the room is bringing real analytical rigor to these gyms. That's the gap. That's why I'm here.

## What I believe

The thing I believe most is this: **data doesn't replace culture. It defends it.**

The reason to look hard at your numbers is not to become a different kind of business. It's to stay the business you are, long enough to keep doing what you're doing.

The owner still owns the floor. The owner still makes the calls. The owner still decides who the gym is for and who it isn't for and what the culture is. None of that changes because you started measuring things correctly. What changes is that the decisions you're already making become decisions you can trust.

You'll know which lead source is actually worth your time, so you can stop spending money on the ones that aren't.

You'll know what your real close rate is, so you can see when it drops before it becomes a problem.

You'll know whether that promo worked, not whether it felt busy.

You'll know which members are actually sticking and which ones are leaking, so you can fix retention before it shows up in the P&L.

You'll know enough to run your gym like the serious business it already is.

The people who win this next decade are the owners who protect what's authentic about their gyms while running them like real businesses. Not one or the other. Both. The culture is the product. The data is the tool that keeps the product alive.
